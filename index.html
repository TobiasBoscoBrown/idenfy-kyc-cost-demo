For most fintechs, automated identity verification wins on every axis that matters. It clears a user in seconds instead of 18+ minutes, costs a couple of dollars instead of hundreds, and catches more fraud because it pairs document checks with biometrics and liveness. Manual review still has a place for edge cases and high-risk escalations, not for the bulk of your onboarding.

Why onboarding speed is now a revenue problem

Verification used to be a back-office checkbox. It isn't anymore. When a new user is sitting in your sign-up flow with their phone in hand, every extra minute is a chance to lose them. The data is blunt about it: 70% of financial institutions say they have lost prospective clients because onboarding was too slow or too complex, a figure that climbed from 67% in 2024 and just 48% in 2023.

The drop-off clusters right at the identity step. By industry estimates, roughly one in five applications is abandoned over KYC and document friction, and around 70% of users walk away if verification drags past three minutes. So the question isn't whether to verify. It's how to verify fast enough that the user you worked hard to acquire actually makes it through.

Where manual review still earns its keep

Automation is the default, not the whole answer. Keep humans in the loop for the cases that genuinely need judgment: a flagged high-risk jurisdiction, a politically exposed person match that needs context, a document that looks tampered in a way the model wants a second opinion on. The pattern that works is automate the 95% and escalate the rest, so your specialists spend their hours on the 50 cases that matter rather than the 5,000 that don't.

Frequently asked

How long should identity verification take?
For a smooth flow, aim for under a minute end to end. Automated document plus biometric checks return a result in seconds, while manual review averages 18 minutes or more. Since roughly 70% of users abandon if verification passes three minutes, speed is a conversion lever, not just an operations metric.
How much does KYC cost per customer?
Fully loaded manual review runs $25 to $50 in analyst time per check, and more than half of corporate and institutional banks report $1,500 to $3,000 per review once compliance overhead is counted. Automated verification is closer to a dollar or two per check, which is why high-volume onboarding moves to automation first.
Is automated KYC less accurate than a human?
For standard cases the opposite tends to hold. Pairing document verification with face matching and liveness detection catches manipulations a human skimming a scan can miss, and it applies the same standard to every check. Humans stay valuable for nuanced escalations, not bulk review.
What is the difference between KYC, KYB, and AML?
KYC verifies an individual customer's identity. KYB does the same for a business, including its owners and structure. AML is the broader anti-money-laundering program that screening, monitoring, and reporting feed into. Identity verification is the front door for all three.
Can automated verification keep us compliant across regions?
A capable platform supports thousands of document types across jurisdictions and produces a time-stamped, exportable audit trail for regulators. That coverage is hard to staff manually, which is part of why automation also lowers compliance risk.
